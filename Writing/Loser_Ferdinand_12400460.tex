\section{Theoretical Background}
\label{sec:theory}

We define our Partially Linear Model for a given city $i$ at time $t$ as follows:

$$Y_{it} = \theta_0 D_{it} + g_0(X_{it}) + U_{it}, \quad E[U_{it}\vert{}D_{it}, X_{it}] = 0$$

$$D_{it} = m_0(X_{it}) + V_{it}, \quad E[V_{it}\vert{}X_{it}] = 0$$

Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $Y_{it}$ is the natural logarithm of transport $CO_2$ emissions.
    \item $D_{it}$ is the policy treatment indicator (e.g., Congestion Pricing).
    \item $X_{it}$ is the high-dimensional matrix of city-level covariates.
    \item $\theta_0$ is the true causal parameter of interest.
    \item $g_0(X_{it})$ is the true expected baseline emissions given city characteristics.
    \item $m_0(X_{it})$ is the true propensity score (the probability of policy adoption).
    \item $U_{it}$ and $V_{it}$ are the respective error terms.
\end{itemize}

Our nuisance parameters are defined as $\eta = (g, m)$, with their true values being $\eta_0 = (g_0, m_0)$.

\subsection{The Orthogonal Score Function}

To estimate $\theta_0$ without regularization bias from the machine learning algorithms, we rely on the Robinson (1988) partialling-out approach. We define the moment condition (score function) $\psi$ for a single observation $W = (Y, D, X)$ as:

$$\psi(W; \theta, \eta) = (Y - \theta D - g(X))(D - m(X))$$

The true parameter $\theta_0$ uniquely solves the population moment condition $E[\psi(W; \theta_0, \eta_0)] = 0$.

\subsection{Proof of Neyman Orthogonality}

Neyman orthogonality requires that the moment condition is locally insensitive to small estimation errors in the machine learning nuisance functions $\hat{\eta} = (\hat{g}, \hat{m})$.

Mathematically, the Gateaux derivative of the expected score with respect to the nuisance parameter $\eta$, evaluated at the true parameter $\eta_0$, must equal zero in all directions.

Let us perturb the true nuisance parameters by a scalar $r$ in an arbitrary direction $\tilde{\eta} = (\tilde{g}, \tilde{m})$, such that $\eta = \eta_0 + r\tilde{\eta}$. We evaluate the expectation of the score function under this perturbed state:

$$E[\psi(W; \theta_0, \eta_0 + r\tilde{\eta})] = E[(Y - \theta_0 D - (g_0(X) + r\tilde{g}(X)))(D - (m_0(X) + r\tilde{m}(X)))]$$

Substitute $U = Y - \theta_0 D - g_0(X)$ and $V = D - m_0(X)$ into the equation:

$$E[\psi(W; \theta_0, \eta_0 + r\tilde{\eta})] = E[(U - r\tilde{g}(X))(V - r\tilde{m}(X))]$$

Expanding the product yields:

$$E[\psi(W; \theta_0, \eta_0 + r\tilde{\eta})] = E[UV] - r E[U\tilde{m}(X)] - r E[V\tilde{g}(X)] + r^2 E[\tilde{g}(X)\tilde{m}(X)]$$

By the definitions of our DGP, $E[U\vert{}X, D] = 0$ implies that $E[U\vert{}X] = 0$ and $E[UV] = 0$. Similarly, $E[V\vert{}X] = 0$. Applying the Law of Iterated Expectations, the linear perturbation terms vanish:

$$E[U\tilde{m}(X)] = E[E[U\vert{}X]\tilde{m}(X)] = 0$$

$$E[V\tilde{g}(X)] = E[E[V\vert{}X]\tilde{g}(X)] = 0$$

This leaves only the second-order term:

$$E[\psi(W; \theta_0, \eta_0 + r\tilde{\eta})] = r^2 E[\tilde{g}(X)\tilde{m}(X)]$$

Taking the Gateaux derivative with respect to $r$ and evaluating at $r=0$:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial r} E[\psi(W; \theta_0, \eta_0 + r\tilde{\eta})] \bigg\vert{}_{r=0} = \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r^2 E[\tilde{g}(X)\tilde{m}(X)] \right) \bigg\vert{}_{r=0} = 2r E[\tilde{g}(X)\tilde{m}(X)] \bigg\vert{}_{r=0} = 0$$

$\blacksquare$
